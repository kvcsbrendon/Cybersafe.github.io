Cyber Security Threats

Internal Threats

  • Employee Sabotage and Theft
    • Sabotage: Deliberate actions by an employee to disrupt or undermine company operations.
    • Theft: Stealing of physical items or intellectual property by employees.

  • Damage
    • Fire: Can cause extensive damage to property, equipment, and data.
    • Flood: Damages infrastructure, equipment, and data due to water inundation.
    • Power Loss: Disrupts operations and can lead to data loss if backup systems fail.
    • Other Disaster: Poses risks to physical infrastructure and employee safety.
  • Unauthorised Access by Employees and Other Users
    • Secure Areas: Unauthorized entry into restricted areas by employees or other users.
    • Administration Functions: Unauthorized access to administrative controls and settings.

  • Security Levels and Protocols
    • Security Levels: Classification of access permissions based on user roles and responsibilities.
    • Protocols: Established procedures and guidelines for ensuring security and access control.
  • Weak Cybersecurity Measures and Unsafe Practices
    • Security of Computer Equipment and Storage Devices: Inadequate measures to protect computer equipment and storage devices from theft or unauthorized access.
    • Security Vetting of Visitors: Failure to properly screen and vet visitors, allowing unauthorized individuals access to sensitive areas.
    • Visiting Untrustworthy Websites: Accessing websites known for malicious content or phishing attempts, increasing the risk of malware infections and data breaches.
  • Accidental Loss or Disclosure of Data
    • Accidental Loss: Unintentional misplacement or deletion of data.
    • Disclosure: Inadvertent sharing or exposure of sensitive information.
    • Poor Staff Training: Insufficient training of employees on data handling procedures.
    • Lack of Monitoring: Lack of effective monitoring systems to detect and prevent data breaches.

External Threats

  • Malicious Software (Malware)
    • Spyware: Software that secretly gathers information about a user’s activities without their consent.
    • Adware: Software that displays unwanted advertisements to users, often bundled with legitimate software.
    • Ransomware: Malware that encrypts a user’s files or system and demands a ransom for decryption.
    • Malware Malicious software designed to harm or gain unauthorised access to computer systems.
    • Virus A type of malware that can self-replicate and infect other files or systems.
    • Worms Self-replicating malware that spreads across computer networks without user interaction.
    • Rootkits Malicious software that provides unauthorised access to a computer or network while remaining hidden.
    • Trojan Malware disguised as legitimate software, which can perform malicious actions when executed.
    • File-less malware Malware that operates in computer memory without leaving traces on the file system.
    • Crypto Jacking Illegitimate use of a computer’s processing power to mine cryptocurrency.
    • Zero-day threats Security vulnerabilities that are exploited before a patch or fix is available.
    • Keyloggers Software or hardware that records keystrokes, often used to capture sensitive information.
  • Hacking
    • Hacking Hacking refers to the unauthorised access, manipulation, or exploitation of computer systems, networks, or data, often for malicious purposess such as stealing information, disrupting services, or causing damage.
    • Commercial: Unauthorized access to computer systems or networks for financial gain.
    • Government: Intrusions into government systems for espionage or disruption purposes.
    • Individuals: Hacking activities carried out by individuals for various motives, including personal gain or activism.
  • Sabotage
    • Sabotage: Sabotage refers to deliberate actions aimed at damaging or disrupting activities, operations, or systems, often for strategic, ideological, or malicious purposes.
    • Commercial: Sabotage carried out within a business or commercial context, such as tampering with products, disrupting supply chains, or compromising competitors' operations.
    • Government: Sabotage targeting government entities or infrastructure, including acts of espionage, disruption of services, or compromising national security.
    • Terrorism: Sabotage committed by terrorist groups or individuals with the intent to cause fear, destruction, or instability within a society or government.
    • Individuals: Sabotage conducted by individuals for personal reasons, such as revenge, dissatisfaction, or activism, often involving acts of vandalism, destruction, or disruption.
  • Social Engineering
    • Social Engineering: Social engineering refers to the manipulation of individuals to obtain confidential or sensitive information. It involves psychological manipulation and deceit rather than technical exploits. Attackers exploit human vulnerabilities, such as trust or fear, to gain access to information or systems.
    • Phishing: Sending deceptive emails or messages to trick recipients into revealing sensitive information or performing actions that compromise security.
    • Pretexting: Creating a fabricated scenario or pretext to obtain information from individuals or organisations.
    • Baiting: Offering something enticing, such as a free download or USB drive, to lure victims into revealing sensitive information or installing malware.
    • Vishing: Vishing, or voice phishing, involves using phone calls to deceive individuals into providing personal or financial information.
    • Drive-By Attack: A drive-by attack is a type of cyberattack where the attacker accesses a victim's device or network simply by visiting a website or opening an email, exploiting vulnerabilities in software or browsers.
    • Smishing: Smishing, or SMS phishing, is a form of phishing that uses SMS (text messages) to trick recipients into divulging personal or sensitive information or clicking on malicious links.
    • Whale Phishing: Whale phishing targets high-profile individuals, such as executives or celebrities, with the aim of obtaining sensitive information or access to valuable resources within an organisation.
    • Spear Phishing: Spear phishing is a targeted form of phishing where attackers tailor their messages to specific individuals or groups, often using personal information to increase the likelihood of success.
  • Credible Threat Impact
    • Credible Threat Impact encompasses the recognition that the consequences of a credible threat extend across various dimensions, leading to significant adverse outcomes:
    • Operational Loss: This dimension involves disruptions to normal operations, including manufacturing processes, service delivery mechanisms, and the integrity of service-related data. Such disruptions can impede productivity, affect customer satisfaction, and result in downtime.
    • Financial Loss: Credible threats can precipitate financial ramifications, including direct monetary losses such as revenue decline, increased operational costs to mitigate the threat, compensation payments to affected parties, and legal expenses incurred in resolving disputes or addressing regulatory non-compliance resulting from the threat.
    • Reputation Loss: The impact on reputation arises from the erosion of trust and confidence in an organisation due to the perceived inability to adequately address or mitigate the threat. This can manifest as negative publicity, decreased customer loyalty, stakeholder disengagement, and diminished brand value. Furthermore, breaches compromising employee or customer information can exacerbate reputational damage.
    • Intellectual Property Loss: Credible threats pose risks to intellectual property assets, including proprietary information, trade secrets, patents, and copyrighted materials. Unauthorized access or disclosure of such assets can undermine competitive advantages, impair innovation capabilities, and jeopardize strategic initiatives. Additionally, the loss of intellectual property may enable competitors to replicate products or services, diminishing market differentiation.
