Cyber Security Threats

Internal Threats

  • Employee Sabotage and Theft
    • Sabotage: Deliberate actions by an employee to disrupt or undermine company operations.
    • Theft: Stealing of physical items or intellectual property by employees.

  • Damage
    • Fire: Can cause extensive damage to property, equipment, and data.
    • Flood: Damages infrastructure, equipment, and data due to water inundation.
    • Power Loss: Disrupts operations and can lead to data loss if backup systems fail.
    • Other Disaster: Poses risks to physical infrastructure and employee safety.
  • Unauthorised Access by Employees and Other Users
    • Secure Areas: Unauthorized entry into restricted areas by employees or other users.
    • Administration Functions: Unauthorized access to administrative controls and settings.

  • Security Levels and Protocols
    • Security Levels: Classification of access permissions based on user roles and responsibilities.
    • Protocols: Established procedures and guidelines for ensuring security and access control.
  • Weak Cybersecurity Measures and Unsafe Practices
    • Security of Computer Equipment and Storage Devices: Inadequate measures to protect computer equipment and storage devices from theft or unauthorized access.
    • Security Vetting of Visitors: Failure to properly screen and vet visitors, allowing unauthorized individuals access to sensitive areas.
    • Visiting Untrustworthy Websites: Accessing websites known for malicious content or phishing attempts, increasing the risk of malware infections and data breaches.
  • Accidental Loss or Disclosure of Data
    • Accidental Loss: Unintentional misplacement or deletion of data.
    • Disclosure: Inadvertent sharing or exposure of sensitive information.
    • Poor Staff Training: Insufficient training of employees on data handling procedures.
    • Monitoring: Lack of effective monitoring systems to detect and prevent data breaches.

External Threats

  • Malicious Software (Malware)
    • Spyware: Software that secretly gathers information about a user’s activities without their consent.
    • Adware: Software that displays unwanted advertisements to users, often bundled with legitimate software.
    • Ransomware: Malware that encrypts a user’s files or system and demands a ransom for decryption.
    • Malware Malicious software designed to harm or gain unauthorised access to computer systems.
    • Virus A type of malware that can self-replicate and infect other files or systems.
    • Worms Self-replicating malware that spreads across computer networks without user interaction.
    • Rootkits Malicious software that provides unauthorised access to a computer or network while remaining hidden.
    • Trojan Malware disguised as legitimate software, which can perform malicious actions when executed.
    • File-less malware Malware that operates in computer memory without leaving traces on the file system.
    • Crypto Jacking Illegitimate use of a computer’s processing power to mine cryptocurrency.
    • Zero-day threats Security vulnerabilities that are exploited before a patch or fix is available.
    • Keyloggers Software or hardware that records keystrokes, often used to capture sensitive information.
  • Hacking
    • Hacking Hacking refers to the unauthorised access, manipulation, or exploitation of computer systems, networks, or data, often for malicious purposess such as stealing information, disrupting services, or causing damage.
    • Commercial: Unauthorized access to computer systems or networks for financial gain.
    • Government: Intrusions into government systems for espionage or disruption purposes.
    • Individuals: Hacking activities carried out by individuals for various motives, including personal gain or activism.
